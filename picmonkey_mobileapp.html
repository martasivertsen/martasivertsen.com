PicMonkey Mobile App

Lead UX Design & Research . Prototyping . UI Design . 2016

PicMonkey is a often described as a web-based photo editing tool that's as easy to use as Instagram, but as robust as Photoshop. When I joined PicMonkey, I was tasked with designing a mobile app for iOS and Android that continued that theme of easy, robust photo editing. We were a bit behind competitors with releasing a mobile app, so it was important that we move fast, be in parity, and find ways to stand out. I was the sole designer on a team of 9 developers, so I needed to work smart, be efficient, work in an agile way and nothing was precious.

The Challenge

Initially the mobile product was focused around communition, but when I came on board we shifted towards providing a basic but smart photo editing app, keep PicMonkey competitive in the market, and provide our users a quick bridge to picmonkey.com via cloud storage. As we refocused towards photo editing we wanted to stand apart in the market with ability to apply and edit multiple effects in a session and precise effect application via paint on effects.

In the early stages, I pared with director of development to brainstorm daily design concepts sketching ideas on a whiteboard in a quick, loose, and iterative way.
In addition, I set up weekly whiteboard reviews with dev to communicate design direction, answer questions regarding the platform, and hopefully raise red flags before the design was finalized. It was also a way to make design a larger conversation, not just something design owns.

Visualizing those features

After sketching, the next step was to begin visualizing the design concepts with lo-fidelity wireframes and user flows. The goal was to break apart the design from all directions and make sure that it holds up.

Exploring the ui

In tandem with fleshing out the user flows, I also started to explore with specific UI areas to begin to understand how tools might look and function. We wanted a large photo editing canvas, easy tool navigation that would be consistent throughout all tools, and light weight home view from which to jump into the editor.

Prototyping and testing

In order to finalize the and test the user experience, instead of doing flat mocks I created prototypes in tools like Origami or Principle to continue proving or disproving design concepts.

Gathering user feedback

With these prototypes I would gather adhoc feedback from the team or from employees in other areas at PicMonkey. Though this isn't a traditional user feedback loop, it was important to gather some bit of input outside of myself without slowing down the development process.

Key stakeholders were devided in what should happen after an user taps save in the editor. Should they stay in the editor or should they return to the camera roll where they first opened the image. I decided that this would be an important flow to test. For this particular study I asked the participants to imagine and sketch what they would expect or want to see after tapping "Save".

Applying a visual language

I worked on establishing a visual language for PicMonkey mobile that built off of Branding from PicMonkey.com, but also would work for iOS and Android platforms. The Creative Director was a great source for applying the brand and a way that worked for mobile. Areas to consider were tools, UI, modals, and components.

Key takeaways

The final product was a fun, light weight tool, with stickers and text surprising the team with their popularity. Analyzing analytics also showed that users spent a significant amount of time in session photo editing. Though we deemed the launch as successful, we had a vocal minority of current PicMonkey.com users that were frustrated the mobile app's features were not in parity with PicMonkey.com. One of those features that was missing was Touch Up, which would become the next product feature. What I would do differently is gather user input early to what features would they want to see in this app.

There are many ways to approach design

There’s the ideal UX approach that you learn in school and then there’s the real world where things can get a little messy. In my work I try to find the middle ground, pulling from past experiences to build an approach that’s flexible, inclusive, and transparent.

When beginning a new project I always take some time to find clarity on the problem, the user, the business goals, and the market. It’s important to collect as much relevant information as possible to shape the project moving forward.

Understanding the problem

After having some bumpy starts and unclear project goals, I realized that my first step before starting design work should be to clearly understand the problem I am trying to solve. This step involves working with product to clarify business goals and define user pain points that we are hoping to improve.

Market analysis

Before diving into a new project I like to take time to look at the market. An analysis could just be screen shots to hang on a wall or a formal document to share with the broader team. The goal is to understand what’s working and what’s missing in the market.

User data

Depending on the timeline, this step may involve gathering quick analytics or a more extensive project working in tandem with a researcher to create, send, and analyze survey data. At this stage any information that I can glean about the user and their intentions or desires is invaluable.

Solidify feature list

Before moving into design, I like to create a simple document that outlines everything above and clarifies what features will be included in this project. This is the document that I will review with the team before moving on.

Once there's clarity on the project, I begin fleshing out the design. At this point I always start in low fidelity, which helps keep focus on the problem not the visuals. During the iteration phase I take time to solicit feedback from other designers, product stakeholders and development.

Sketch and brainstorm

Before moving to the computer I always start with sketches. It’s important to think big picture, dive into questions about the platform, and get ideas from different perspectives.

User journeys

When working on a new design, it’s useful to think about how the user will flow through the new feature from start to end. User journeys are always part of the iteration phase and help to catch strange experiences or edge cases.

Prototypes

As I map out the user journey I will build prototypes along the way. Prototyping has become essential to my design process, as the digital world has become more interactive. The in-between moments can help or hinder a user experience and prototypes test those moments.

Review

I set up frequent check-ins during the iteration phase as critical components to improving the design. I enjoy working on teams where it's common to have quick desk side reviews with other designers throughout the day. It's also important to involve development in weekly reviews where we go through updates, get feedback and answer questions. The dev reviews are also a place for product and other key stakeholders to observe and get up to speed.

Test

When a prototype is ready or the design is closer to final, it's a great time to gather user feedback through usability testing. If traditional testing is not feasible I often do adhoc tests with other employees outside of the team. Gathering feedback and gaining a perspective into how the solution is being received and understood is critical to designing an intuitive product.

Finalize

Before moving into development, I gather specifications into a final document that will be the source of truth, a resource to come back to, and a place for remote team members to get information quickly.

I’ve found that it's more efficient if design is accessible and available during development. I’m here to answer questions, solve problems that were missed, pivot designs that can’t be built, and test development in progress. This phase may often move in tandem with the iterate phase, which allows the team to move faster and waste less time.

Communicate

It’s important to review and communicate final design direction. I like to make sure we are on the same page as the development team in what we are attempting to build and to answer any further questions before kicking off development.

Test

While development is in progress I think it’s important for design to test and go through the latest builds. This is a chance to check the user experience, give feedback, and raise red flags.

Iterate

Design is a moving target and during the development phase changes will need to be made.
